title: Autonomous Wheelchair Platform
category: hardware
timeframe: "2021"
status: completed
summary: Developed a smart wheelchair customized from a commercially available manual chair, featuring dual control modes with joystick navigation and hands-free autonomous routing through indoor environments.
stack:
  - Stereo depth camera
  - LIDAR
  - Nvidia Jetson processor
  - DC gear motor & controller
  - Computer vision
  - Embedded control
  - Sensor fusion
  - Mobile application
impact:
  - Created low-cost, lightweight solution accessible to broader user base beyond wealthy demographics.
  - Demonstrated autonomous navigation through indoor hospital facilities and general indoor locations.
  - Published open-source design files enabling public reproduction and broader accessibility.
  - Provided dual control options with manual joystick and hands-free autonomous navigation.

context: "Powered wheelchairs cost $15,000–$30,000 and autonomous models even more, pricing out the majority of people who need them. This project started at Clarkson University to prove that autonomy could be added to a standard manual wheelchair at a fraction of the cost."
problem: "Mobility-impaired users in hospitals and indoor facilities often cannot navigate independently. Existing autonomous wheelchair solutions are prohibitively expensive and not open-source."
constraints: "Must use a commercially available manual wheelchair as the base. Total cost must remain accessible. Must work in real indoor environments with obstacles. Must offer both manual and autonomous control."
approach: "Integrated stereo depth camera, LIDAR, and Nvidia Jetson for perception and path planning. Added DC gear motors for propulsion. Built a mobile app for route selection. Used sensor fusion to handle dynamic obstacles in real time."
outcome: "Successfully demonstrated autonomous indoor navigation in hospital-like environments. Published two IEEE papers and open-sourced all design files for reproduction."
lessons: "Accessible technology doesn't need to be cheap technology — it needs to be thoughtfully designed. Starting with a commercial wheelchair base dramatically reduced mechanical complexity and kept the focus on the intelligence layer."
